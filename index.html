Muriwai Coastal Environment

NCEA Level 3 Geography (91426) - Excellence Mastery Dashboard

Setting the Scene: The MCE

Muriwai is a rugged, high-energy coastline located 42 km west of Auckland. While the beach stretches 50 km from Otakamiro Headland to the Kaipara Harbour, our specific case study area focuses on a 3-5 km stretch from Okiritoto Stream in the north, down to Otakamiro Point and Maukatia (Māori) Bay in the south.

The Five Key Elements

Elements are the "ingredients" of the environment that processes act upon:

1. Geology (Rock)

The southern end features Manukau Breccia (a hard, erosion-resistant volcanic conglomerate formed 17 million years ago). North of the headland, softer sandstone and siltstone have been eroded away to form the bay.

2. Sediment

The beach consists of fine, black titanomagnetite iron sand (0.25mm diameter) sourced from Taranaki and central North Island volcanoes, mixed with lighter quartz and feldspar.

3. Waves

High-energy waves with a massive fetch of 2,000 km across the Tasman Sea. They average 1.5 m in height with an energy of 250,000 to 500,000 joules per m³.

4. Wind

Prevailing winds are southwesterly/westerly (onshore). They blow over 18 km/h 42% of the time, providing immense energy for both waves and aeolian transport.

5. Vegetation

Crucial for dune formation. Includes native spinifex and pingao, as well as introduced marram grass acting as biological baffles.

Key Natural Processes

To gain Excellence, you must understand how these processes operate as a sequence of logical steps.

A. Marine (Wave) Processes

  • Wave Refraction: As waves approach the irregular coastline, the shallow water around Otakamiro Headland causes the waves to slow down due to friction. The wave crests (isobaths) bend, concentrating wave energy (orthogonals) onto the headland (increasing erosion) and dissipating energy into the bays (allowing deposition).
  • Hydraulic Action / Pressure: High-energy waves smash into the cliff, trapping and compressing air inside rock fissures. When the wave recedes, the air expands explosively, shattering the rock.
  • Abrasion (Corrasion): Waves hurl rocks, pebbles, and heavy titanomagnetite sand against the cliff face, wearing it away like sandpaper.
  • Longshore Drift: Prevailing SW winds cause waves to approach the beach at an oblique angle. The swash carries sediment diagonally up the beach, while gravity pulls the backwash straight down (at a 90° angle). This zig-zag motion transports roughly 250,000 m³ of sediment northwards annually.

B. Aeolian (Wind) Processes

  • Deflation & Entrainment: During low tide, the 150m wide beach is exposed to the sun, drying the sand and making it friable (loose). Strong SW onshore winds exceeding the fluid threshold velocity of 5.47 metres per second (20 km/h) pick up the sand.
  • Saltation: The primary method of wind transport. Sand grains bounce in a skipping motion along the beach face. As they land, they dislodge other grains, creating a chain reaction.
  • Aeolian Deposition: As the wind hits dune vegetation, it creates a zone of zero wind velocity (about 10 cm high). The wind loses energy, dropping the sand, which leads to dune accretion (growth).

C. Sub-Aerial Weathering

  • Spray-splash Weathering: Wave splash reaches high up the cliff face. When water evaporates, salt crystals are left in the rock's pores. As these crystals grow, they exert pressure, flaking the rock into a "honeycomb" pattern.
  • Water-layer Weathering: Tidal changes leave saltwater ponds on flat shore platforms. The sun evaporates the water, and the resulting salt crystals erode the surface downwards.

🌊 How Interacting Processes Shape Features

In Geography 3.1, you must explain how processes interact (how the output of one process becomes the input for another, or how they work together).

Example 1: Formation of Dunes

  1. Wave Deposition brings titanomagnetite sand onto the beach.
  2. The sun dries the sand during low tide, making it friable.
  3. Aeolian Transportation (Saltation) is triggered by strong onshore SW winds (>5.47 m/s), moving sand inland.
  4. Vegetation Growth interrupts the wind, creating a zero-velocity zone causing Aeolian Deposition. The plants bind the sand, forming stable Dunes.

Example 2: Evolution of Otakamiro Cave

Volcanic activity 17m years ago created the resistant Manukau breccia. Tectonic action lifted the headland above sea level 10,000 years ago, making it vulnerable.

Waves approaching the headland slow down in shallow water, causing them to refract, concentrating massive energy on the end of the headland.

This energy drives corrasion and corrosion. The most important sub-process is hydraulic action, where water is forced into a crack and expands due to pressure differentials to force the crack apart. Together these act on weaker points until a cave forms.

Sustained Spatial Contrasts at Muriwai

Spatial variation refers to distinct differences in features, elements, or the operation of processes across different geographical locations at a single point in time.

Macro Variation: The Headland vs. The Embayment

The core geographic argument is establishing a hierarchy of processes: The Marine Process is the paramount driver of spatial variation because wave refraction dictates both the erosional intensity at the headland and the depositional sediment supply for the beach.

🔍 E8 Deep Dive: Micro Spatial Variations

These examples demonstrate how interacting natural processes operate differently across various locations within the Muriwai environment, resulting in distinct physical features.

1. Sand Dune Formation (North vs. South)

Northern Dunes (Okiritoto): The interaction between wind deposition and spinifex vegetation creates lower, parabolic-shaped dunes around 6 to 8 metres high with a gentle 20-degree slope. Spinifex grass sends out horizontal runners, spreading the deposited sand over a wider area.

Southern Dunes (Headland): The interaction between wind deposition and marram grass creates much steeper (45-degree slope) and higher dunes reaching 12 to 15 metres. Marram grass grows straight upwards through the sand, forcing vertical dune growth. These steep dunes are highly vulnerable to wave undercutting.

2. Shore Platform Erosion (Inner vs. Seaward Edge)

Inner Platform: Water-layer weathering dominates this zone. As the tide retreats, pooled salt water dries in the sun, forming salt crystals that loosen rock fragments. The next high tide washes these fragments away, gradually eroding the inner platform down to a height of 2 metres.

Seaward Edge (Rampart): The outer edge remains at a height of 3 metres because constant wave splash prevents it from drying out. The lack of drying prevents salt crystal formation, effectively protecting the rampart from water-layer weathering.

3. Longshore Drift Direction (Northward vs. Southward)

Normal Coastline: For 95% of Muriwai Beach, Longshore Drift operates in a northward direction due to the prevailing SW winds, maintaining a beach width of about 100m.

Variation at the Headland: In the 500m stretch immediately north of Otakamiro Headland, wave refraction causes the waves to bend completely around the point, hitting the beach from a northwesterly angle. This creates a localised southward longshore drift. The headland acts as a barrier, trapping this southward-moving sand and building a much wider beach (approx. 200m wide) in this specific area.

Temporal Variations at the MCE

Temporal variation refers to the differences in how natural processes operate across different periods of time, ranging from seasonal shifts to long-term geological timeframes.

⏳ E8 Exemplars: Timeframes of Change

These examples explain how interacting processes change over different time periods, from seasonal shifts to geological epochs.

1. Seasonal Wave Action (Summer vs. Winter)

☀️ Summer (Constructive): High-pressure anti-cyclones generate calmer winds and lower-energy constructive waves (<100,000 joules/m³, <1.5m). These waves possess a strong swash and a weak backwash, allowing them to deposit sediment and build up the beach to a steeper gradient (5–10°) with a distinct berm.

⛈️ Winter (Destructive): Low-pressure storm systems generate fierce winds and massive, high-energy waves (>400,000 joules/m³, >3m). The powerful backwash drags sand off the beach, stripping the berm away. This leaves a flatter, dissipative beach profile (0–2.5°) and forms offshore sandbars, allowing storm surges to cause severe dune scarping.

2. Seasonal Aeolian (Wind) Transport

☀️ Summer Transport: Warmer, drier weather dries out the deposited sediment quickly. Dry sand is much lighter and does not stick together (making it friable), rendering it highly susceptible to wind transport via saltation, suspension, and surface creep.

⛈️ Winter Transport: Rain and heavy clouds leave the coastal environment damp. Wet sand binds together, significantly raising the threshold wind velocity required to move it. Consequently, wind transportation operates at a very slow rate or completely halts during winter.

3. Historical Variations (1950s vs. Present Day)

1950s (Erosion Dominant): Human activities (Maori burning, European farming, grazing) stripped dunes of vegetation. Without a 'zone of zero wind velocity', aeolian deposition stopped. Severe aeolian erosion (blowouts) occurred, migrating sand up to 5 km inland.

Present Day (Deposition Dominant): The Auckland Council implemented extensive planting programs (marram, spinifex). Vegetation growth successfully interacted with aeolian transport, dramatically increasing deposition. Today, stable foredunes range from 3m to 12m high.

4. Long-Term Geological Change (1 Million Years Ago vs. Today)

1 Million Years Ago: Following volcanic activity roughly 17 million years ago that formed the Piha Conglomerate rock, tectonic uplift eventually exposed the Otakamiro Headland to marine erosion for the first time around 1 million years ago.

Today: The continuous interaction of wave refraction, hydraulic action, and corrosion has drastically reshaped the headland. Refracting waves concentrate massive energy on the headland, while hydraulic action forces air into fissures to shatter the rock. Corrosion uses this broken rubble to further wear down the rock face, ultimately carving out caves, arches, and isolated features like the Motutara Stack over thousands of years.

Conversely, winter cyclonic systems generate high-energy plunging waves (>400k joules/m³) whose powerful backwash erodes the beach to a dissipative 0–2.5° profile, while high precipitation binds sand grains, raising the fluid threshold velocity and completely stalling aeolian transport."

Teacher Note: Masterful contrast. Integrates deep mechanics (cohesion, fluid threshold velocity, reflective/dissipative profiles) and exact data to prove the temporal shift.

How an Excellence (E8) Essay Looks

Analysis of Excellence Exemplar: Geography 91426. Examiner reports consistently highlight that successful candidates plan their responses to ensure logical construction, and explicitly focus on the interactions between processes.

Structure Breakdown

Paragraph 1: Introduction Case study - specific location. Key natural process – and interacting natural processes – identified. Declare a clear thesis.
Paragraphs 2-3: The Process Key natural process (and 4 sub-processes like hydraulic action, corrasion, etc) described in detail with reference to the case study diagram.
Paragraphs 4-6: The Interaction Interaction of key natural process with three other natural processes described in detail (e.g. Saltation interacting with Vegetation Succession) with close reference to the case study and a second diagram.
Paragraph 7: Shaping The role of natural processes in shaping the specific variations (spatial or temporal) in the case study environment.
Paragraph 8: Conclusion Bringing it all together – summarising how interacting natural processes impact the case study context.

Detailed Annotations are Critical

Do not just label "Headland." Annotate it:

"Otakamiro Headland: Composed of resistant Piha Conglomerate rock, subjected to high-energy wave refraction."

1. The "Base Map" Strategy

Memorise one simple outline: a straight beach at the top (North), jutting out into Otakamiro Headland with Motutara Stack just offshore, curving down into Māori Bay (South).

  • For Spatial Variation: Draw SW winds (58%). Show LSD moving 175,000 m³ north. Contrast headland (erosion) vs beach (deposition). Contrast dunes (Spinifex north vs Marram south).
  • For Temporal Variation: Split the map or use a dual key. Summer: calm weather, constructive waves, steep 5-10° gradient. Winter: cyclonic, destructive waves, flat 1-2° dissipative profile.

2. Wave Refraction Diagram (Zoom on Headland)

Draw parallel wave crests approaching deep water. As they hit shallow water, draw them bending. Annotate orthogonals (arrows perpendicular to crests) converging on the headland (Hydraulic Action) and diverging into Māori Bay.

3. Aeolian Deposition (Cross-Section)

Draw ocean, wide foreshore, and rising dunes. Annotate the source (drying foreshore), the transport (Saltation bouncing, Suspension, Surface creep at >18km/h winds), and the deposition (vegetation creating a "zone of zero wind velocity").
